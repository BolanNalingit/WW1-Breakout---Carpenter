THE GAS ATTACK ORDER
☁ CHEMICAL WARFARE · YPRES · 22 APRIL 1915
SOLDIER DIARY · PRIMARY SOURCE
Private William Hay, 1st Canadian Division — Ypres, April 22, 1915
"...About five o'clock in the evening, a strange yellow-green cloud appeared along the German line, rolling toward us slowly. It burned the throat and made your eyes stream with tears. Men fell to the ground gasping. We had no protection — none had ever been issued. The order came from the medical sergeant: urinate on your cloth and press it to your face. It was all we had.

The date — 22 April 1915 — will live in infamy as the first large-scale use of poison gas in warfare. Nearly six thousand cylinders of chlorine were released by the Germans along a four-mile front at Ypres. The cloud stretched six kilometres wide."
— Diary of Pte. William Hay, Canadian Expeditionary Force Archives, 1915 [PRIMARY SOURCE]
WAR OFFICE DIRECTIVE · PRIMARY SOURCE
Gas Directive No. 7 — Anti-Gas Precautions, November 1915
"All troops are required to carry the Phenate Helmet at all times in forward positions. The helmet was standardized after the Second Battle of Ypres, where German forces deployed 168 tonnes of chlorine gas. Troops must note: the gas alarm signal in all British sectors is six short rings followed by a long ring. Protective helmets must be donned within eight seconds of the alarm sounding..."
— British War Office, Gas Directive No. 7, November 1915 [PRIMARY SOURCE]

THE TANK DIRECTIVE
⚙ ARMOURED WARFARE · FLERS-COURCELETTE · 1916
OFFICER DIARY · TANK CORPS · PRIMARY SOURCE
Lt. Basil Henriques, Tank Corps — Battle of Flers-Courcelette, Sept. 15, 1916
"We moved before dawn through the mud — a terrifying, roaring machine unlike anything that had existed on a battlefield. I commanded D17, one of the 'Mark I' tanks. The German infantry simply ran. They had never seen such a beast.

Our crew of eight men crawled at no more than four miles per hour, but the psychological effect was devastating. Of the 49 tanks sent forward, only 32 actually reached their starting positions. The rest were lost to mechanical failure."
— Diary of Lt. Basil Henriques, Tank Corps, Imperial War Museum, London [PRIMARY SOURCE]
WAR OFFICE MEMORANDUM · LANDSHIPS COMMITTEE · PRIMARY SOURCE
Technical Specifications — His Majesty's Land Ship, June 1915
"The design submitted by William Foster & Co. has been approved for production under the codename 'Water Carrier' — chosen to deceive enemy intelligence intercepting supply manifests. Upon protest by committee members who objected to the initials 'WC', the vehicle was instead renamed 'Tank' — a reference to the steel water storage containers the vehicle resembled when crated for transport.

The Mark I weighs 28 tons, carries two 6-pounder guns and four Hotchkiss machine guns, and requires a crew of eight. Maximum armour: 12mm."
— War Office Landships Committee Memorandum, June 1915, National Archives UK [PRIMARY SOURCE]

🗺 TRENCH WARFARE · SOMME · 1916–1917
FIELD NOTEBOOK · ROYAL ENGINEERS · PRIMARY SOURCE
Sgt. Thomas McBride, RE — Trench Construction Orders, Somme, August 1916
"The standard British trench system runs in three parallel lines: the Front Line, the Support Line, and the Reserve Line. Connecting them are diagonal 'communication trenches' cut at angles to prevent enfilading fire.

The Front Line follows a zigzag traverse pattern — each straight section not to exceed ten yards. Our section at Grid Reference SOMME-7 was ordered to dig to a depth of six feet, with a firestep cut at two feet from the bottom..."
— Field Notebook of Sgt. Thomas McBride, RE, Liddle Collection, University of Leeds [PRIMARY SOURCE]
GHQ OFFICIAL MANUAL · PRIMARY SOURCE
"Notes on Trench Warfare" — British GHQ, 1917
"The term 'No Man's Land' refers to the unoccupied ground between opposing front-line trenches — average width varied from 50 to 250 metres. The Front Line was also termed the 'Fire Trench.'

The innermost defensive position was the Reserve Line, located approximately four hundred yards behind the front. The Reserve Line was never to be abandoned without explicit written orders from a general officer..."
— "Notes on Trench Warfare," British GHQ, 1917. National Archives WO 158/311 [PRIMARY SOURCE]

1. From Sgt. McBride's notebook — the depth trenches were ordered dug
2. From the GHQ Manual — the name of the innermost defensive line

Combine in order: [DEPTH] [LINE NAME]  eg. FIVE FEET FRONT LINE

THE AERIAL CIPHER
✈ AERIAL WARFARE · RFC · 1917–1918
RFC COMBAT REPORT · PRIMARY SOURCE
Capt. Arthur Roy Brown, No. 209 Squadron RFC — April 21, 1918
"...At approximately 10:45 hours I observed a red Fokker triplane diving on Lt. May's machine at low altitude over the Somme. I immediately dived and opened fire. The enemy aircraft crashed near Vaux-sur-Somme.

The pilot was positively identified as Manfred von Richthofen, holder of the Pour le Mérite and credited with 80 aerial victories — the highest-scoring fighter ace of any nation in the war. His aircraft, painted entirely red, was known to Allied pilots as the 'Red Baron.'"
— Combat Report, Capt. A. Roy Brown, No. 209 Sqn RFC, April 21 1918. National Archives AIR 1/2388 [PRIMARY SOURCE]
RFC COMMUNIQUÉ No. 83 · PRIMARY SOURCE
Aerial Reconnaissance Policy Directive — HQ Royal Flying Corps, April 1917
"Reconnaissance aircraft are not to cross enemy lines below 5,000 feet without fighter escort. Since 1916, RFC cameras have produced over 430,000 photographs of enemy positions, proving decisive at Arras and Vimy Ridge.

The observer must call out enemy positions using the clock system: enemy at three o'clock means target to the right of the aircraft heading. All reports to be filed on RFC Combat Report Form W3348 within two hours of landing..."
— RFC Communiqué No. 83, April 1917, Royal Flying Corps HQ [PRIMARY SOURCE]
